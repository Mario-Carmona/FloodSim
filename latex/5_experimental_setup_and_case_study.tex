\chapter{Experimental Setup and Case Study}
\label{chapter:experimental_setup_and_case_study}

This chapter details the geographic and computational configuration established to validate the tensor-based flood simulation engine. 
By defining the spatial boundaries, discrete resolution, and material parameters, we establish a framework capable of executing efficient 
hydrodynamic predictions.

\section{Case Study Setup: DANA 2024 and Geographic Delimitation}
\label{section:case_study_setup_dana_2024}

The catastrophic "DANA" event that struck Valencia (Spain) on October 29, 2024, serves as our primary evaluation scenario. Characterized by torrential rainfall, it triggered violent flash-flooding along the Barranco del Poyo and the Turia River basin. The active simulation domain covers a bounding box of $1311\text{ km}^2$, tracking the floodwave from the upper catchments of Chiva and Cheste through "ground zero" municipalities (Paiporta, Sedaví, Alfafar) to the Mediterranean coast.

\subsection{Domain Discretization and CFL Stability Condition}

The simulation domain is discretized into a uniform raster grid using a cell size of $\Delta x = 200\text{ m}$. While the Spanish National 
Geographic Institute (IGN) provides Digital Terrain Models (MDT) at 5, 25, and 200 meters, the $200\text{ m}$ resolution was selected as an 
optimal trade-off. It successfully captures the critical terrain furrows and natural channels followed by the floodwave while drastically 
reducing the computational weight. This allows the model to run efficiently on free online cloud servers without requiring days of 
computation, while still delivering highly accurate results.

To guarantee numerical stability, the execution engine enforces a fixed temporal step of $\Delta t = 0.15$ seconds to satisfy the 
\textbf{Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condition}. In explicit numerical schemes for fluid dynamics, the CFL condition dictates that information 
cannot propagate through the grid faster than the physical time-step allows. Mathematically, it requires the Courant number ($C$) to be 
less than or equal to a stability threshold (typically $1$):

\begin{equation}
    C = \frac{v_{\text{max}} \cdot \Delta t}{\Delta x} \le 1 \implies v_{\text{max}} \le \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}
\end{equation}

Because $\Delta t$ is fixed, it must be calibrated for the absolute worst-case scenario: a combination of maximum terrain slope 
($S_{\text{max}}$) and maximum water depth ($WD_{\text{max}}$) yielding the highest potential physical velocity. With 
$\Delta x = 200\text{ m}$ and $\Delta t = 0.15\text{ s}$, the maximum numerical velocity supported by the grid is:

\begin{equation}
    v_{\text{max}} = \frac{200\text{ m}}{0.15\text{ s}} \approx 1333.33\text{ m/s}
\end{equation}

This setup easily satisfies the CFL criteria. Even under extreme, catastrophic water depths (e.g., 
$WD > 10\text{ m}$) and steep slopes where physical velocities could reach up to $30\text{ m/s}$, the combined speed remains orders of 
magnitude below the $1333.33\text{ m/s}$ numerical limit, ensuring absolute stability throughout the execution.

\subsection{Physical Fluid Properties and Regime Selection}

The simulation parameterizes standard water constants via global scalars: fluid density ($\rho = 1000.0\text{ kg/m}^3$) and dynamic 
viscosity ($\mu = 0.001\text{ Pa}\cdot\text{s}$). The computational engine applies a minimization gating function, 
$v_{\text{approx}} = \min(v_{\text{turb}}, v_{\text{lam}})$, to select between a laminar viscous velocity and a turbulent open-channel 
velocity (based on surface friction). Due to the low viscosity denominator ($\mu$), the theoretical laminar path yields extremely high, 
non-physical values under flash-flood conditions. This automatically forces the computational graph to bypass the laminar path and route 
all active grid computations exclusively through the turbulent friction regime, ensuring physical consistency across the floodplain.


\section{Hydraulic Scale and Color Representation}

To ensure quantitative readability across all bi-dimensional and tri-dimensional visual outputs, a standardized discrete 
color scale consisting of 15 intervals was defined. This chromatic framework is conceptually grounded in the classic 6-level 
flood hazard classification established by the Australian Emergency Management Institute and utilized by 
\cite{Smith2016} for estimating flood fatalities and identifying populations at risk based on vulnerability 
curves.

While the reference framework relies on six broader categories—utilizing critical thresholds such as $H5$ to define severe 
structural and life-safety risks (see Figure \ref{fig:original_hazard} for the original reference curves)—the scale proposed 
in this work expands these thresholds into 15 high-resolution intervals. This sub-discretization does not contradict the 
underlying physics of the original curves; rather, it introduces a tighter granularity specifically tailored to capture the 
full socio-environmental spectrum of this specific event. It filters out negligible hydraulic noise below 0.20 m and transitions 
smoothly from bright yellows to deep reds, magentas, and dark purples, allowing the visual outputs to clearly differentiate 
standard urban street flooding from the ultra-catastrophic submersion levels (exceeding 12.0 m) observed in the study area.
